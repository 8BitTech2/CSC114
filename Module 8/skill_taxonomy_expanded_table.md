## What changed from v1

- **Course crosswalk added** — each category now names the specific FTCC courses that cover it, rather than relying on the classifier to infer this from course descriptions alone.
- **Certification and occupation crosswalks added** — ties each category to concrete, citable external standards (useful for the "Workforce Standards, Certifications, and Gray Literature" literature theme).
- **Keyword lists expanded**, particularly for `cloud_platforms` and `security_compliance`, to capture more phrasing variants.
- **Classifier bug found and fixed during this expansion**: `cloud_platforms` was scoring 3.5/100 curriculum coverage in the original pipeline despite CTI-141 being weak-labeled correctly. Diagnosis: only 1 of 74 training documents was a positive example for that category, and `LogisticRegression` without class balancing suppressed its predicted probability to near-zero even for the true positive. Adding `class_weight="balanced"` raised CTI-141's predicted probability from 0.03 to 0.89, and curriculum coverage for the category from 3.5 to 34.4.
- **Second, larger correction from an official document**: FTCC's `2025SU_IT_CROSSWALK.pdf` (Computer Technologies Certificate Crosswalk) shows that CTI-240 and CTI-241 — described generically as "virtualization administration" in the NCCCS course text — actually align to the **AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate** and **Microsoft Azure Administrator (AZ-104)** exams respectively. Course descriptions alone never mention AWS/Azure by name, so the classifier had no way to detect this from description text alone. Adding certification names as additional weak-labeling signal per course raised `cloud_platforms` curriculum coverage from 34.4 to 70.6 — a correction grounded in an authoritative FTCC document, not an inferred fix.
- **Third, largest correction: curriculum scoring now uses expert-coded ground truth, not classifier predictions.** The instructor hand-coded all 15 courses against all 9 categories (`courses_aligned_with_pillars.xlsx`). Comparing this against the automated classifier's own weak-labels gave a Cohen's kappa of 0.073 ("slight" agreement) — 84 of 144 cells disagreed, almost entirely in the direction of the classifier under-counting real coverage. `apply_taxonomy.py` was rebuilt so curriculum-side scoring comes directly from the expert matrix; the classifier is now only used for the O*NET job-task side (where no expert coding exists), and is trained on the expert labels instead of keyword weak-labels for its curriculum-side training data.
- **Hardware/physical infrastructure flagged as a genuine (not artifact) weak category** — only one course (NOS-110) touches it directly, and it remained among the lowest-scoring categories through all three corrections.

## Still not done

- **This expert-vs-classifier comparison is not the same as a true inter-rater reliability check.** It compares one human's judgment against an algorithm, not two independent humans against each other and the taxonomy's category boundaries. A second human rater independently applying the coding manual is still the outstanding step for validating the taxonomy itself (see `taxonomy_coding_manual.md`).
- Course crosswalks were built from the real NCCCS course descriptions gathered earlier in this project, not from a formal content analysis — worth strengthening before this table goes into the dissertation as a validated instrument rather than a working artifact.
